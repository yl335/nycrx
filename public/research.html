SBHC Reproductive Health Project

Using 340B to help to reduce teenage pregnancy in NYC.

NYCRx has partnered with NYC Department of Health, NYC Department of Education and Fund for Public Health in New York, with support from an anonymous donor, to provide free contraceptives at school-based health centers (SBHC) in NYC. We helped each 340B eligible SBHCs implement the 340B program so that they could purchase contraceptives at deeply discounted prices, and save the anonymous donor a significant amount of money.

The program was supposed to last 3 years program. Fortunately, due to NYCRx’s leveraging of the power of the 340B program, the donor was able to stretch the funding to 5 years. Year 5 has just begun and NYCRx has saved ____ to date.

Our hope is that by having access to safe contraceptives, teens can avoid unwanted pregnancies, focus on their education and build a solid foundation for their futures.

Differential Drug Pricing in NYC

Determining if prescription access is affected by location and neighborhood factors.

In partnership with SUNY Downstate School of Public Health, NYCRx has investigated how drug pricing and availability change from location to location through the five boroughs of NYC.

The main research questions are:

  • Does drug pricing correlate with neighborhood income?
  • Do certain neighborhoods have greater or lesser access to affordable medication?

This project may shed light on how drug pricing is influenced by an area's socioeconomic, political and geographical environment and thus add a new dimension to improving access to affordable medications for people who need it the most.

In 2011, we helped SUNY Downstate School of Public Health with the research methodology, study design and data collection. The report on the findings is nearing final draft form.

Medication Adherence in Patients with Type II Diabetes

Studying methods to improve medication adherence in patients with Type II Diabetes.

In partnership with Touro College of Pharmacy, NYCRx is working with an inter-professional team to research and improve medication adherence in patients with type II diabetes in Central Harlem communities.

Central Harlem has one of the highest rates of type II diabetes across the five boroughs, and thus improving medication adherence will significantly improve the health of a large portion of people living in Central Harlem.

In 2011, we did the literature review, put together the study design, and compiled a well-rounded research team. In 2012, we hope to secure funding for this project, run the study at a Central Harlem-based clinic, report on the findings and recommend interventions to improve medication adherence.

Our belief is that taking medication in a consistent and timely manner, along with a healthy lifestyle, can improve the welfare of patients with type II diabetes while managing healthcare costs.

New York State Medicaid “Carve-in” Study

Understanding the impact of Medicaid Managed Care inclusion in 340B at clinic level.

Historically, New York State Medicaid has not allowed prescriptions to be included in the 340B program. However, in October 2011, NY State Medicaid allowed eligible clinics to optionally include Medicaid Managed Care prescriptions into the 340B program (“the carve-in”). While other states have also enacted carve-ins, no study to date has highlighted the impact of a carve-in at the clinic level.

The purpose of this project – conducted in partnership with New York University’s Robert F. Wagner School of Public Service – is to report on the impact of the carve-in by using pre and post carve-in data from select participating clinics in NYC.
